Ring of Kerry Drive

Killarney activity review • Alternative full-day option

Snapshot

The Ring of Kerry is a 179 km (111-mile) circular driving route around the Iveragh Peninsula in County Kerry — Ireland's most famous scenic drive. The route follows the N70 and N71 through a constantly shifting landscape of rugged Atlantic coastline, mountain passes, colorful villages, and ancient stone forts. Key stops include Killorglin (home of the ancient Puck Fair), Cahersiveen (gateway to Valentia Island and the Skellig Experience), Waterville (Charlie Chaplin's beloved holiday retreat), Derrynane (Daniel O'Connell's ancestral home), Sneem, and Kenmare before returning to Killarney via Moll's Gap and Ladies' View. Tour buses travel counter-clockwise by convention, so independent drivers going clockwise can enjoy a slightly less congested experience in early September.

Key facts

Route179 km loop from Killarney, counter-clockwise via Killorglin, Cahersiveen, Waterville, Sneem, Kenmare, Moll's Gap
Drive time3.5–4 hours of pure driving. With stops: 7–9 hours for a full day.
CostFree (it's a public road). Individual attraction entries along the way vary.
DurationFull day: depart 8:30–9:00 AM, return 5:00–6:00 PM
AccessibilityMostly in-car. Viewpoint pull-offs are generally flat. Individual attractions vary.
Best conditionsClear day essential for views. Early September is excellent — fewer tour buses than peak summer, heather in bloom on the hillsides.

Why we'd want to do this

  • It's the most iconic drive in Ireland — the one everyone back home will ask about.
  • The variety is unmatched: ocean cliffs, mountain passes, sandy beaches, stone forts, and colorful villages all in one loop.
  • With two cars, the group can split and stop at different things based on interest.
  • Valentia Island is accessible via a short bridge and offers views toward the Skellig Islands without the demanding boat trip.
  • Kenmare is one of Ireland's most charming towns — a perfect lunch stop.

What's good to see

  • Cahersiveen — The Old Barracks heritage center (in a striking former RIC barracks) and the gateway to Valentia Island.
  • Valentia Island — Cross the bridge for views of the Skelligs, the Skellig Experience visitor center, and the Tetrapod Trackway (385-million-year-old fossilized footprints).
  • Waterville — Charlie Chaplin's summer retreat. His bronze statue on the seafront is a classic photo stop. Butler Arms Hotel for coffee.
  • Derrynane House — The home of Daniel O'Connell, "The Liberator," set on a stunning beach. The house tour is 45 minutes and illuminating for history lovers.
  • Sneem — Postcard-perfect village with two squares, colorful houses, and excellent craft shops.
  • Kenmare — The "jewel of the Ring." Excellent restaurants (The Horseshoe, Mulcahy's), a stone circle, and charming streets.
  • Moll's Gap and Ladies' View on the return to Killarney — among the finest viewpoints in Ireland.

Common review themes

What reviewers love

  • "The most beautiful drive I've ever done" is the dominant theme across thousands of reviews.
  • Self-driving gives total flexibility — reviewers consistently prefer it over bus tours.
  • Early September is praised as the ideal time: fewer crowds, good weather odds, autumn light.
  • Kenmare consistently gets singled out as a highlight — "we wished we'd stayed a night there."

Common complaints

  • It's a very long day. "We underestimated how tiring 7+ hours of driving and stops would be."
  • The roads are narrow in places, especially when meeting tour buses on blind corners.
  • In poor weather, many of the viewpoints are socked in — "we drove all day and saw fog."
  • Some reviewers feel it's "too much driving, not enough doing" compared to spending a full day in the National Park.

Try something different — alternative plans

If the Ring of Kerry feels too long a day in the car, three alternatives that capture similar scenery:

⛳For the golf fans

Dooks Golf Club — links on the Ring

A hidden gem links course in Glenbeigh, right on the Ring of Kerry route. Founded in 1889, it sits between Dingle Bay and the mountains. Green fees around €80–100. One group member could play while the others continue the Ring, meeting up in Kenmare for lunch. Book at dooksgolf.com.

🍴For the foodies

Half-Ring + Kenmare food trail

Drive only the southern half of the Ring (Killarney → Moll's Gap → Kenmare), skip the full loop, and spend the afternoon in Kenmare exploring its food scene: The Horseshoe for modern Irish, Mulcahy's for seafood, and No. 35 for wine and cheese. A more relaxed day with better eating.

🗺For the sightseeing fans

Skellig Experience + Valentia Island

Instead of the full Ring, drive directly to Cahersiveen and Valentia Island (90 min from Killarney). Visit the Skellig Experience visitor center for the story of the Skellig monks, see the Tetrapod Trackway, and take in views of Skellig Michael from the cliffs. Return via Waterville. Half the driving, more time at each stop.

Round 1 — Opening views

Ireland Specialist
Here's my honest take: the Ring of Kerry is spectacular, but for this itinerary I'd make it the alternative, not the default. The group already has Muckross, Torc, Ladies' View, and Moll's Gap on Thursday — they're seeing the best of Kerry's scenery already. The Ring adds 7+ hours in the car for views that overlap significantly.
Group Logistics
I agree. A full Ring of Kerry day means no midday hotel rest. You're in the car or at a viewpoint from 9:00 AM to 5:00 PM. That conflicts with the group's preferred pace. You could do a half-Ring — Killarney to Kenmare via Moll's Gap, lunch in Kenmare, then home — as a gentler version.
Food & Culture Lead
If they do the full Ring, Kenmare is the must-stop. The food there is genuinely excellent — better than anywhere else on the route. And Waterville has a charm that photos can't capture. But I'd still prefer Dingle for the food-and-culture balance.
Accessibility Advisor
The Ring is mostly an in-car experience, so it's physically easy. The issue is fatigue from a long day. If the group does it, build in a proper 45-minute lunch and at least one 20-minute coffee stop. Don't try to see everything — pick 4–5 stops max.

Round 2 — Back-and-forth

Ireland Specialist
If the group insists on the Ring, go clockwise. The tour buses go counter-clockwise, so you'll have clearer roads and arrive at each viewpoint without the big groups. September is ideal for this — buses thin out after August.
Group Logistics
With two cars, one could do the full Ring while the other does Dingle or the Gap of Dunloe. That gives everyone their preference. Regroup at the hotel for dinner.
Food & Culture Lead
If splitting: the Ring car should stop at Derrynane House (history) and Kenmare (food). The Dingle car gets Dick Mack's and Out of the Blue. Both have a great day.
Accessibility Advisor
Derrynane House is accessible on the ground floor. The beach walk at Derrynane is sandy but hard-packed enough for confident walkers. Beautiful spot.

Revisions agreed by the panel

  • Position the Ring of Kerry as a strong alternative, not the default Friday plan.
  • If done: go clockwise, depart by 8:30 AM, limit stops to 4–5, and aim to return by 5:00 PM.
  • Prioritize Valentia Island, Waterville, Derrynane, Sneem, and Kenmare as stops.
  • Consider a half-Ring (Killarney → Moll's Gap → Kenmare → return) as a gentler option.
  • Two-car split — one car Ring, one car Dingle — is an excellent strategy for this group.

Panel's final recommendation

The Ring of Kerry is world-class scenery, but the panel rates Dingle as the better Friday plan for this group's pace and interests. The Ring works best as a car-split option: one car does the Ring (clockwise, 4–5 stops, Kenmare lunch) while the other does Dingle. If the whole group goes together, choose Dingle. If they split, the Ring car will have a spectacular day.
