Dublin

Saturday, August 29 — Monday, August 31, 2026

Dublin is over 1,000 years old — founded as a Viking settlement around 841 AD — yet it's one of Europe's youngest cities, with nearly half its population under 30. You'll feel that energy the moment you step outside: Georgian doorways painted every color imaginable, buskers on Grafton Street, and a pub on almost every corner. The area around Trinity College and the River Liffey is compact enough to walk end to end in 30 minutes, and nearly everything on our list is within that radius. Expect world-class whiskey, live traditional music that starts without announcement, and locals who will talk to you whether you want them to or not.

Sunday 8/30 — Full Day

The main event. Morning history, midday Guinness, afternoon whiskey, evening trad session.

03 Morning

Trinity College & the Book of Kells

Trinity College is Ireland’s oldest university (1592) and houses the Book of Kells — a 9th-century illuminated gospel manuscript that is Ireland’s most prized cultural treasure.

04 Midday

Guinness Storehouse

A 7-story purpose-built visitor experience inside the St. James’s Gate brewery complex where Guinness has been brewed since 1759. Gravity Bar pint at the top, lunch at the 1837 Brasserie.

05 Afternoon

Irish Whiskey Museum

Not a distillery — a dedicated museum and tasting venue on Grafton Street. 4-whiskey comparative tasting, 60 minutes. 3-minute walk from hotel.

06 Evening

The Cobblestone — Traditional Music Session

A plain-spoken public house in Smithfield run by the Mulligan family for four generations. Widely regarded as the single best trad-music pub in Dublin. Arrive 9:00 PM.

Gastropub in Stoneybatter with a serious kitchen — craft beer, whiskey-focused drinks list, and food well above pub standard. Locals-heavy and relaxed, 10-minute walk from The Cobblestone. ~20–30 EUR per main. Book for 6:30 PM.

A poitin-focused cocktail bar on Green Street in Smithfield, around the corner from The Cobblestone. Ireland's original spirit, once illegal, now revived in a beautiful space with serious cocktails. The Dublin speakeasy equivalent — one or two rounds before the trad session.

A fully digital, interactive museum in the vaults of Custom House Quay. Tells the story of how 10 million people left Ireland. Swap in for the Whiskey Museum if anyone has Irish ancestry to trace.
