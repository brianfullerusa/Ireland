Kehoe's

Dublin activity review • Sunday 8/30 alternative evening pub

Snapshot

A genuine Dublin institution on South Anne Street, just off Grafton Street. Kehoe's is everything a Dublin pub should be: small, dark, Victorian, unchanged. No music, no food, no televisions — just pints, conversation, and an interior that hasn't been renovated since the mid-20th century. The front bar has a mahogany counter, frosted glass partitions, and wooden snugs that feel like private rooms. After-work crowds of young professionals pack the place on weekday evenings, but Sunday nights are quieter and more atmospheric. One of the most authentic "local" pub experiences available in central Dublin.

What it is

Kehoe's has been a pub since the 1800s, but what makes it special is what hasn't happened to it. While hundreds of Dublin pubs have been gutted and modernized, Kehoe's remains almost exactly as it was in the mid-20th century. The front bar is divided by frosted glass partitions into semi-private sections, each with its own character. The mahogany counter is worn smooth by generations of elbows. The snugs — small enclosed booths with their own doors and service hatches — are among the last functioning examples in Dublin.

There is no music in Kehoe's. There is no food. There are no screens showing sport. The pub's entire proposition is the space itself and what happens inside it: people drinking pints and talking. On a Sunday evening, with the after-work crowds gone, Kehoe's settles into its most atmospheric state — quiet enough to hear conversations at the next table, dark enough to lose track of time, and warm enough to make you want to stay for one more round.

South Anne Street connects Grafton Street to Dawson Street, putting Kehoe's in one of the most accessible locations in Dublin. It pairs naturally with The Pig's Ear on nearby Nassau Street for a city-center Sunday evening — dinner at the restaurant, then a 3-minute walk to Kehoe's for a pint before heading to Smithfield for the trad session.

Common review themes

What reviewers love

  • "This is what a real Dublin pub feels like" is the dominant review sentiment.
  • The snugs are consistently singled out as a highlight — private, intimate, atmospheric.
  • Reviewers appreciate the absence of music and screens — it forces conversation.
  • The Guinness pour is praised as one of the best in Dublin.
  • Sunday evenings are described as particularly atmospheric — quieter, more local.

Common complaints

  • Gets extremely crowded on Thursday and Friday evenings — packed to the door.
  • No food at all — come fed or don't come.
  • The interior is narrow and tight — difficult to navigate when busy.
  • No outdoor seating.
  • Toilets are basic — functional but nothing more.

Try something different — alternative plans

If Kehoe's isn't the right Sunday evening pub, consider:

🍺Similar vibe, different spot

The Stag's Head on Dame Court

Dublin's most beautiful Victorian pub interior — stained glass, carved mahogany, mosaic floors. Same no-music, no-food philosophy as Kehoe's but in a more visually dramatic setting. Also 5 minutes from the hotel.

🥃Cocktails instead of pints

Bar 1661 in Smithfield

If the group would rather have cocktails than pints between dinner and The Cobblestone, skip Kehoe's and go directly to Bar 1661 in Smithfield. Poitin cocktails, fascinating history, and steps from The Cobblestone.

🗺Skip the pub, head north

Taxi straight from dinner to The Cobblestone

If the group is running late or wants to maximize time at the trad session, skip the intermediate pub stop entirely. Taxi from The Pig's Ear to Smithfield — 10 minutes — and arrive at The Cobblestone by 8:30–9:00.

Round 1 — Opening views

Ireland Specialist
Kehoe's is one of my favorite pubs in Dublin. It's not famous for any one thing — it's famous for being exactly what a Dublin pub is supposed to be. No frills, no pretense, just a beautiful old room where people drink and talk. On a Sunday evening it's at its best.
Food & Culture Lead
If you want to understand Dublin pub culture, Kehoe's is where you learn. The absence of music and food is the point — the pub is the entertainment. Order a Guinness, sit in a snug if one's free, and listen to the room. That's it. That's the experience.
Accessibility Advisor

Round 2 — Back-and-forth

Ireland Specialist
Finding it: walk down Grafton Street, turn left onto South Anne Street. Kehoe's is on the right side — a narrow pub front with "Kehoe's" above the door. You'll see the frosted glass and mahogany through the window.

The snugs are first-come, first-served. If one's available, take it — they're a piece of Dublin history. The small doors and service hatches date from an era when certain customers didn't want to be seen in a pub. Clergy, women, professionals — the snug offered privacy.
